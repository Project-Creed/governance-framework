# Governance Writing Guide

## Purpose

The Governance Framework is a practical guide to citizenship, representation, lawmaking, policy, taxation, budgeting, administration, elections, public goods, emergency powers, foreign policy, technology, and constrained public power. It extends Ethosism into the moral use of collective decision-making.

The book should help a reader answer: who may decide, for whom, by what authority, under what limits, with what evidence, at what cost, through what institution, with what accountability, and toward what public inheritance?

## Core Commitments

Every chapter should connect back to five commitments:

1. Public trust: governing power exists in trust for the people affected by it, not as private possession.
2. Reality: policy must answer to facts, tradeoffs, incentives, budgets, capacity, and consequences.
3. Reciprocity: rules must remain defensible when roles are reversed among majority, minority, taxpayer, dependent, official, dissenter, and future citizen.
4. Constraint: authority must be limited by law, rights, procedure, transparency, and review.
5. Inheritance: governance should preserve and improve the institutions, resources, liberties, and public goods future people will depend on.

## Moral Method

Ground claims in five checks:

1. Objective reality: what problem exists, what evidence supports it, what tradeoffs follow, and what capacity is available?
2. Reciprocity: would the rule remain fair if you were governed by it from the weaker, dissenting, minority, dependent, taxpayer, or future position?
3. Integrity: do public promises, incentives, procedures, budgets, enforcement, and outcomes align with the stated standard?
4. Constraint: what rights, laws, checks, transparency, review, and limits prevent abuse?
5. Long-term responsibility: what does this governance pattern become across years, decades, and generations?

Do not treat governance as mere power, majority will, expert management, private gain, symbolic performance, or permanent emergency. The standard is legitimate public decision-making under reality, reciprocity, constraint, accountability, and inheritance.

## Theology And Politics Stance

The Governance Framework is secular and non-theological. It should be usable by atheists, agnostics, and religious readers.

The book may discuss citizenship, elections, parties, taxation, budgets, public services, regulation, rights, administration, corruption, emergencies, technology, sovereignty, and foreign policy, but it should not become a partisan program. It may name real failures: corruption, capture, incompetence, dishonesty, disorder, waste, rights violations, bureaucratic cruelty, irresponsible debt, emergency abuse, factional contempt, and institutional decay. It should reason from consequences, reciprocity, authority, constraint, tradeoffs, competence, public trust, and long-term inheritance rather than party identity or culture-war signaling.

## Voice

Write in a direct essay style. The tone should be serious, practical, and morally clear without becoming theatrical, cynical, bureaucratic, technocratic, populist, utopian, or ideologically smug.

Prefer:

- Concrete claims.
- Plain language.
- Clear distinctions between public trust, private preference, majority will, rights, administration, enforcement, and accountability.
- Concern for competence without technocratic arrogance.
- Concern for popular consent without mob rule.
- Concern for constraint without paralysis.
- Concern for public goods without fiscal denial.
- Nuance without evasion.

Avoid:

- Partisan commentary.
- Electioneering.
- Anti-politics cynicism.
- Bureaucratic excuse-making.
- Technocratic control.
- Populist theater.
- Constitutional slogans without application.
- Policy abstraction detached from budgets, incentives, administration, and tradeoffs.
- Overstated certainty where facts, jurisdictions, capacities, and consequences vary.

## Chapter Pattern

Most chapters should follow this shape:

1. Define the governance domain and why it matters.
2. Identify the common failure mode.
3. State the Governance standard for that domain.
4. Show how the standard follows from reality, reciprocity, constraint, accountability, competence, and long-term public trust.
5. Address tradeoffs, edge cases, or predictable objections.
6. End with a concise behavioral expectation or decision standard.

Do not force this pattern when a chapter needs a different structure, but keep the chapter moving from principle to practical judgment.

## Practice Blocks

Domain chapters should leave the reader with a usable practice. Include a short practice section or equivalent closing application that covers:

1. Plain standard: what the chapter asks the reader to take seriously.
2. Reality test: what problem, evidence, capacity, cost, and tradeoff exist.
3. Reciprocity test: what role reversal requires.
4. Authority test: who may decide and under what limits.
5. Accountability test: what transparency, review, correction, or consequence is owed.
6. Constraint test: what rights, procedures, budgets, or institutional checks prevent abuse.
7. Long-term test: what this governance pattern becomes across years, decades, or generations.
8. First practice: one concrete behavior the reader can change this week.

The practice should make the essay actionable without turning governance into slogans.

## Consistency Checks

Before finishing a chapter or revision, check:

- Does the chapter depend on theology, partisan identity, revolutionary romance, technocratic authority, or anti-politics cynicism? If so, rewrite it in secular, nonpartisan terms.
- Does it explain why the governance standard is good, not merely assert it?
- Does it respect role reversal for majority, minority, taxpayer, dependent, official, dissenter, outsider, and future citizen?
- Does it account for evidence, budgets, incentives, competence, administrative capacity, public trust, and predictable consequences?
- Does it distinguish public trust from private possession, consent from manipulation, expertise from rule by experts, and emergency from blank-check power?
- Does it connect to transparency, accountability, constraint, rights, duties, and long-term public inheritance?
- Does it avoid excusing corruption, incompetence, rights violations, fiscal evasion, institutional capture, public disorder, or symbolic performance?
- Does it sound like Project Creed prose: clear, sober, concrete, and adult?
